Giving Up Tech Metaphors

Published on July 4, 2022 by David VanDusen.

I learned to use English among academic, artsy, native speakers. Our lexicon was erudite. [The words we used showed off how well-read we were.] However, using rare, metaphorical, hyperbolic, and euphemistic words just doesn’t work for me anymore.

Instead of trying to impress my friends with my vocabulary, I’m trying out another way of choosing words. Nowadays, I use English with casual, genuine, non-native speakers. Our goal is to communicate, especially at work. However, there are a lot of metaphors and euphemisms that are common among technologists, and that makes that harder.

I’m trying to use fewer metaphors to be better understood. One risk of choosing not to use common metaphors is that I might have to use more words, and my listener might wonder why I’m not using the common expression. Since I’ve started practicing, that hasn’t been a problem. There’s always a better thing to say.

Here are some examples of changes I’m making. Some words are offensive, some don’t convey any additional information, and some are just incorrect.

Quality Assurance

When I used to talk with people about “Quality Assurance”, I’m not sure any of us knew what we were actually talking about. We didn’t know how quality was being measured, nor how we were assuring it. If we were being honest, we could have said, “Trying it out in staging.”

Now I’m more likely to talk about “looking for edge cases”, or “trying to break it”. I don’t think a formal sounding word like “Quality Assurance” or even something more correct like “defect detection” helps in the day-to-day situations when technologists talk about this process.

Sanity check

When I used to say “sanity check”, I was talking about checking something, but it never had anything to do with psychology. Without adding confusion or more words, I’ve said “consistency check”, or even just “check” and been fully understood.

Nuke

The imagery of “nuking” some code that has been problematic is exciting, and horrific. If I want to communicate that something will be completely removed, now I’ll say “complete remove” instead of invoking the horrors of war.

I’ll continue to document phrases and alternatives here as I try them out. 💬
